Design Notes

Typography: Extra-bold condensed sans-serif at massive scale. 3D extrusion with depth shading. Scanline/halftone horizontal line texture layered over gradient fills (blue-to-dark). Letterforms are architectural — they become environments, not just text.

Illustration style: Flat vector with strong outlines. Characters are abstracted/surrealist — basketball-headed figures, floating eyeballs, red geometric humanoids. No photorealism; everything is graphic and symbolic.

Composition: Dense, layered, collage-like. Objects overlap without strict depth hierarchy. Energy comes from contrast between: dark voids vs. bright solids, rigid geometry vs. organic figures, static letterforms vs. dynamic characters.

Texture: Neon grid perspective lines (retro-80s arcade). Diamond/gem motifs signal rarity and value. Star/sparkle accents scattered throughout to imply magic and premium.

Relevance for OTT2/Halo: Strong model for combining sport + digital collectible aesthetics. The dark-first palette, bold type hierarchy, and animated card reveals are directly applicable to a streaming/highlights product that wants a premium, energetic feel.

02 — Design Critique of NBA Top Shot Queue
Alvin Chan  ·  Product Designer  ·  Medium ↗  ·  March 31, 2021 · 16 min read
UX Critique Queue / Waitlist Live Drop UX Social Proof Sound Design NFT / Collectible First-Time Users

Context: In-depth product design critique of the NBA Top Shot pack drop queue by a product designer and active user. Covers the full pack drop funnel — announcement → waiting room → random queue assignment → checkout — with a focus on first-time user experience and trust in the randomization system.

Product Hero — Moments as 3D Cards

Visual language: Moments are presented as physical 3D trading cards in a dark space with neon outlines. Each card shows the player name, team logo badge, action (dunk/layup), and series. Floating/rotating in space signals digital collectibility vs. a flat image. Neon blue/purple outlines give a premium, hi-tech feel against the dark background.

Moments Marketplace — Card Listing UI

Card data hierarchy: Player Name → Play type + Date + Series → Rarity (Common #/10000 LE) → Badges → Lowest Ask price → Number of listings. Clean light-mode card component. Badge system (team + sponsor icons) adds visual layering for authentication and identity. Price is the dominant action driver.

Pack Drop Page — Purchase UI

Layout: Split-screen — pack 3D visual left, purchase details right. Dark background with high-contrast "PRE-ORDER" label (cyan on dark). Rules listed with bullet icons inside a bordered box. Large price display, single dominant CTA button ("JOIN PRE-ORDER DROP"), secondary text link below. Scarcity reinforced: "Only 1 pack per person."

Queue Flowchart — Pack Drop Process

Drop funnel: Announcement (>8h) → 15min buffer → Random queue assignment → User checked if they can get a pack → Pack reserve → Checkout via Dapper. The key moment of uncertainty is the random assignment (~60 seconds) — this is the core UX problem the article addresses.

Queue Experience — "You Are Now In Line" Screen

Queue UI anatomy: Black background. Bold white "YOU ARE NOW IN LINE" heading (trust signal / confirmation). Subtitle identifies the specific pack. Blue progress bar (indeterminate — no real progress indication). Counter: "Number of users in line ahead of you: 7608" with timestamp. "LEAVE THE LINE (You will lose your place)" — clear consequence warning. Queue ID for support. This UI is minimal but cold — no engagement, no excitement, no emotional buffering during the wait.

Proposed UX Concepts

1. Randomizer Animation Show numbers spinning and landing — like a lottery draw. Makes the fairness of the system visible and visceral. Reduces suspicion that the queue is biased.
2. Sound Design Non-diegetic audio during queue: riff, build-up, celebration cue. Three triggers: New Badges / Queue Randomizer / Pack Opening. Music softens the anxiety of waiting and reinforces excitement of outcome.
3. Social Proof Queue Show neighboring users in the queue (username + position). Creates "in this together" effect — Asch conformity principle. Seeing others in the same position builds trust and reduces drop-off.

Key UX Insights — Relevance for Halo / OTT2

User Psychology

Users of high-scarcity digital products ask: "Is this even legitimate?" and "Is the system biased against me?" Design must proactively answer these questions without the user having to ask. Transparency and visible fairness are table stakes in any competitive digital-access flow.

3 Design Principles (First-Time Users)

1. Make it clear what you want me to do.
2. Tell me what to expect and give me feedback.
3. Minimize friction and reduce effort to a minimum.

These apply directly to any "access event" in OTT2 — live game start, exclusive content drop, highlight reveal.

Target User Segments

Basketball Fans · Sports Community · Digital Asset Newcomers · Scalpers · Investors. The article focuses on newcomers to digital assets as the highest-opportunity segment — they convert if onboarded well, but drop hardest if confused. Directly maps to Halo's challenge: bringing casual sports viewers into a new digital product.

Motion & Sound as Trust Tools

The article proposes that animation + sound = perceived fairness. A spinning number feels more random than a static one. Music during uncertainty reduces frustration (Psychology of Music in Gambling Environments research). For Halo: micro-animations and sound cues during loading, buffering, or access moments could meaningfully reduce churn.

03 — NBA Team Sites — Design System
Object × Engine Digital × NBA  ·  2022  ·  object.design ↗
Design System Color Tokens Branded Experience Roster Page Team Theming 30 Teams / 1 System Sponsorship

Context: Object + Engine Digital partnered with the NBA in 2022 to rebuild all 30 team websites with a single unified design system that adapts to each team's brand identity. The core challenge: one system, 30 color palettes, 30 brand personalities — consistency without uniformity.

Hero — System Overview

⬤ Branded Experience — Color Tokens per Team

The key insight: Each team's brand colors are mapped to a semantic token set — primary, secondary, surface, on-surface, accent — and then applied globally across every component. The result: the same template reads as a Lakers page (purple/gold), a Celtics page (green), a Hornets page (teal/purple). The design feels native to each team without rebuilding anything. This is exactly the token architecture relevant to Halo/OTT2.

Brand Assets — Token System in Motion

Token architecture: The GIF shows the design token system in action — the same component set cycling through different team themes. Every color, surface, and text role is a token, not a hardcoded value. Changing the team = swapping the token set. UI elements screen shows the component library: buttons, labels, score chips, nav items, cards — all inheriting from the token layer.

Page types: The system covers Home, Schedule, Scores, Standings, Roster, Stories, and Sponsorship pages — all rendered through the same token-driven component library.

⬤ Roster Page

Why it works: The Roster page is the most visually rich section — player photography is the primary content, and the token system lets each team's brand color become the backdrop. Player cards use a large cutout photo, name, position, stats. The mobile version adapts to a vertical scroll list. The "action" and "bio" variants show how a single player's card expands into a full profile.

Courtside — Live Score & Pregame

Live states: The same score widget has three states — Pregame (schedule + ticket CTA), Live (real-time score with team colors pulsing), Final. All states use team color tokens so they feel native even in a shared widget. This is directly relevant to any Halo live-game or highlights experience.

Sponsorship Integration

Design Notes — What's Worth Taking

✓ Color Token Architecture

One component library, 30 themes. Each team's palette is expressed as a semantic token layer (primary, secondary, surface, on-surface, accent). Zero custom code per team — only token values change. For Halo: the same architecture can serve multiple sports, leagues, or broadcast partners from a single component system.

✓ Roster Page Pattern

Large player photography as primary visual anchor. Team brand color as background fill (not a decorative choice — it's structural). Name + position + number in high-contrast type. Card → expanded bio transition. For Halo: strong model for any player-centric or talent-centric content surface.

✗ What Doesn't Apply

The typography and layout feel relatively safe/editorial — not as bold or energetic as the El Dorado NBA Top Shot work. The brand expression is restrained by NBA's official brand guidelines. Halo has more freedom to be expressive. Use the architecture, not the aesthetic.

Live Score Widget Model

Pregame → Live → Final state machine as a single themed widget. Each state has a distinct visual weight and CTA (Watch / Score / Stats). The widget inherits team color tokens so it always feels brand-native. Strong pattern for any OTT2 live game or event card component.

Design Technique — Glow as Form

🌱
Bioimpedance
Large half-sphere, top-heavy. Multi-layer aurora: acid green center → teal mid-ring → deep blue outer haze. The "planet from space" energy. Bottom strip glows blue like an atmosphere.
⚡
Charger
Lightning bolt as a gradient object — volt yellow at tip, gradients to green at base. Defined by contrast against pure black. No border, no shadow. Just colored light.
❤️
Heart Rate
Heart shape as cutout from solid black. Magenta→red glow radiates from the edge inward. Negative space as the subject. Typography is muted #888, never competes with the shape.

UX Insights — Relevance for Halo / OTT2

① Team Color Glow on Player Cards
Rather than filling a player card with flat team colors, use them as luminous glow layers — team's primary color emitted from behind the player silhouette. This is exactly the "color token per team" approach Isaac wants, but expressed cinematically.
② Drop Moment = Glow Trigger
When a highlight drops or an athlete is claimed, the card could pulse with a radial glow in team color — exactly the energy of the "Charger" icon. The glow intensity = moment significance. Pure black → burst of color → settles.
③ Athlete Silhouette as Negative Space
The "Heart Rate" technique — player silhouette as a cutout from black, with team color glowing from the edges inward. This makes an unclaimed athlete feel like a void waiting to be filled, and a claimed one feels like they're radiating energy.
④ Dark Mode = Default, Not Toggle
This aesthetic only works on true black backgrounds. The glow technique collapses on white or even dark gray. This is the strongest argument for Isaac/Roy's position: dark mode isn't a preference, it's a design precondition.

UX Insights — Relevance for Halo / OTT2

① Live Match Tab Pattern
The Live / Statistics / Line-Up / Stats tab bar directly maps to Halo's game companion view. The "Join ›" CTA inside the score widget is a clean model for the live fan engagement trigger — enter the game context with one tap.
② Discover / Following = The Drop Loop
The TikTok-style vertical feed with Discover + Following toggle is exactly Isaac's engagement loop made real. Discover = algorithm surfaces highlights. Following = graph-personalized content. Both needed for Halo's identity graph to pay off.
③ Onboarding via League Selection
The league selection grid is the first graph enrichment moment — before users see content, they signal their preferences. This feeds the identity graph immediately. IBBA onboarding should mirror this: pick your teams first, then everything becomes personalized.
④ Profile as Gamified Stats Dashboard
Post Views / Items Found / My Games creates a light gamification layer without leaderboards. For Halo: "Highlights Viewed / Players Claimed / Games Attended" — the athlete claiming mechanic expressed as a user identity, not just a feature.
⑤ Deep Navy vs. Pure Black — A Critical Distinction
This app uses #0B1628 (deep navy) rather than pure black — warmer, more "premium sports broadcast" feel vs. the cold, editorial black of REF 04. For Halo, this is a brand decision: if the team color tokens inject warmth, deep navy BG works. If team colors are the only warmth, pure black keeps the glow effect intact. Both are valid — they're different product personalities.

Drop Reveal — Stacked Card Fan

Motion Design Concept · "ALL PURPOSE / ASPECT TEST" · Pinterest

ALL PURPOSE
ASPECT TEST
Motion Breakdown
① Stack State — Entry
Cards sit flat and stacked — only the top card (entry thumbnail) is visible. This is the resting state the user taps into.
② Fan Out — Reveal
Cards spread from center-bottom pivot, rotating in 3D space in both directions — like a hand of playing cards opening. Each card is a content artefact: a highlight screenshot, an athlete poster, a stat card.
③ Hold — Read the Room
Fan is held open long enough for the eye to scan all cards. User perceives depth and richness — "there is a lot here." This is the value moment.
④ Collapse — Choose or Enter
Cards snap back to stack. The thumbnail is now the "confirmed" entry point. Tap it to enter the Drop/Story.
⊹
Camera HUD Overlay — Aesthetic Language
The original video uses corner brackets, crosshairs, a progress bar, and monospace labels ("ALL PURPOSE", "ASPECT TEST") — a techy, editorial, broadcast-adjacent aesthetic. For Halo this overlay layer is optional but worth noting: it reads as "professional sports production system," which reinforces the platform-as-infrastructure narrative Isaac is building. Could appear during loading/processing states.
① Drop Reveal — Content Richness Signal
The fan-out communicates "this Drop contains multitudes" before the user commits to entering. Unlike NBA Top Shot's single dramatic reveal, this shows a collection — highlights, posters, stats — closer to the NXT Keynote poster wall aesthetic. The imperfection is the point: it's generative, not manufactured.
② NXT Poster Format Connection
Each fanned card can be a full-bleed athlete poster — the kind Halo generates automatically from Pixellot highlight clips. Fan-out becomes a mini NXT-style gallery: multiple shots of the same player moment from different frames, shown simultaneously before the user picks their entry.
③ Identity Graph Trigger
Which card the user's eye lands on during the hold state is a behavioral signal — even without a tap. Eye-dwell or hover data on the fanned cards feeds Isaac's identity graph: the user preferred the stat card over the highlight clip. This is a micro-interaction that enriches the Drop Loop passively.
④ Stack State as Scroll Unit
In the feed/Discover screen, each Drop appears as a stack (not yet fanned). The slight card-edge peeking from behind the thumbnail is enough to signal depth. The fan animates on hover or on scroll-pause — no tap required. This preserves feed momentum while still teasing richness.
⑤ Less Polished = More Authentic — The Halo Advantage
NBA Top Shot's reveal is a one-shot cinematic moment designed for a single collectible. Halo Drops are recurring, algorithmic, and personalized — a cinematic reveal would feel fake after the third time. The card-fan is repeatable, fast (<1s), and scales to any content type. Its slight roughness signals "this was made for you, now," not "this was produced for everyone." That authenticity is a product differentiator, not a limitation.
